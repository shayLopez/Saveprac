Here’s a classic, authentic-style **Bolognese pasta** (inspired by traditional ragù from Bologna, Italy).

---

## Ingredients (Serves 4)

* 2 tbsp olive oil or butter
* 1 small onion, finely diced
* 1 small carrot, finely diced
* 1 celery stalk, finely diced
* 2 garlic cloves, minced
* 500 g (1 lb) ground beef (or beef + pork mix)
* 2 tbsp tomato paste
* 1 cup canned crushed tomatoes or passata
* 1/2 cup dry red wine (optional but recommended)
* 1 cup whole milk
* Salt and black pepper to taste
* 300–400 g pasta (traditionally tagliatelle or pappardelle)
* Freshly grated Parmesan cheese to serve

---

## Instructions

### 1. Prepare the base (soffritto)

Heat the oil or butter in a large pan over medium heat.
Add onion, carrot, and celery. Cook gently for about 8–10 minutes until soft but not browned.
Add garlic and cook for 1 minute more.

### 2. Brown the meat

Add the ground beef. Break it up with a spoon and cook until browned and no longer pink.
Season with salt and pepper.

### 3. Add tomato paste and wine

Stir in the tomato paste and cook for 2 minutes.
Pour in the red wine and let it simmer until mostly evaporated (about 3–5 minutes).

### 4. Add tomatoes and simmer

Add crushed tomatoes. Lower the heat and let it simmer gently for at least 30–45 minutes.
Stir occasionally. The longer it cooks (even up to 1½–2 hours), the deeper the flavor.

### 5. Add milk

Stir in the milk and simmer for another 10–15 minutes. This softens the acidity and makes the sauce richer.

---

## Cook the Pasta

Boil salted water in a large pot.
Cook the pasta until al dente according to package instructions.
Reserve a little pasta water before draining.

---

## Combine and Serve

Add the drained pasta directly into the sauce.
Toss well, adding a splash of pasta water if needed to loosen the sauce.
Serve with freshly grated Parmesan.

---

### Tips for Better Flavor

* Cook it low and slow. Bolognese improves with time.
* Use a mix of beef and pork for a richer taste.
* Avoid too much tomato; traditional ragù is more meat-focused than tomato-heavy.
